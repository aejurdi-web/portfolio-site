## Thesis

Supermicro is a genuine AI infrastructure growth story: Q3 FY2026 revenue grew 123% year over year, and FY2026 guidance of $38.9B-$40.4B implies continued rapid expansion off a $33.7B trailing-twelve-month base. That growth is real, but the valuation framing has to be corrected: once net debt of roughly $7.5B is properly included, the stock's true enterprise value runs closer to $29B-$30B, implying an EV/EBITDA multiple of roughly 16x-18x against TTM EBITDA of $1.6B-$1.8B — a premium to the peer median, not the in-line multiple a market-cap-only read suggests. The investment case therefore rests on that premium being justified by growth and demand visibility, not on a valuation discount waiting to close. Gross margin, which bottomed at 6.3% in Q2 FY2026, recovered to 9.9% in Q3 — an active recovery rather than a permanent floor, though still well below the company's historical range. The Q3 sequential revenue decline of roughly 19% reflects customer site delays pushing deployments into future quarters, not a demand problem; the order backlog and FY2026 guidance both point the other direction. In June 2026, Supermicro also announced a $39B order backlog alongside a $7B equity raise — a combination that signals real demand but also dilution and execution risk through FY2027. A 52-week range of $19.48 to $62.36, paired with a beta of 2.58, shows how violently the market has already re-rated this name in both directions.

Two distinct legal threads also run through the stock — a 2024-25 accounting and auditor crisis that is now resolved, and a separate, unresolved March 2026 DOJ indictment of a co-founder for alleged export-control violations, detailed below. Separately, AI GPU-based platforms account for more than 80% of Supermicro's revenue, making growth directly dependent on NVIDIA GPU allocation and supply — a real concentration risk worth naming explicitly. That said, there is a genuine diversification signal underneath it: enterprise channel revenue grew to 28% of the Q3 total, up from 15% the prior quarter, suggesting demand is broadening beyond hyperscaler/GPU-platform concentration even if the headline numbers remain NVIDIA-dependent for now.

On a corrected basis that properly includes roughly $7.5B of net debt, SMCI's enterprise value runs closer to $29B-$30B, implying an EV/EBITDA multiple of roughly 16x-18x — a premium to the peer median, not the in-line multiple a market-cap-only read implies. This is a different setup than the typical undervaluation thesis: the case rests on growth and demand visibility justifying the premium already embedded in the multiple, not on the market closing a valuation gap.

Applying the peer median EV/EBITDA of roughly 12x-13x (excluding the Pure Storage outlier) to SMCI's current EBITDA would imply an enterprise value of roughly $19B-$23B — well below the $29B-$30B EV the stock currently commands. The bull case rests on forward growth (123% YoY in the most recent quarter, a $39B order backlog, and FY2026 guidance of $38.9B-$40.4B) closing that gap by growing into the multiple, not on current multiples already supporting the valuation.

### Valuation Range

Downside: if the premium compresses to the peer median (~12x-13x EV/EBITDA) on current EBITDA, implied enterprise value of roughly $19B-$23B less ~$7.5B of net debt puts implied market cap at roughly $11.5B-$16B, versus today's ~$22B.

Upside: if FY2026 plays out per guidance ($38.9B-$40.4B revenue) and the market sustains a similar multiple as earnings grow into it, there's room for further upside from current levels.

The width of this range reflects genuine binary risk from the unresolved legal overhang, not analytical imprecision. A tight point estimate would overstate a level of confidence this name doesn't currently warrant.

## Legal & Governance Overhang

<Callout>
This is the third such episode for Supermicro, not the second. An earlier SEC settlement in 2020 resolved accounting matters tied to fiscal years 2014-2017, predating both the 2024-25 crisis and the 2026 indictment below — establishing a recurring pattern rather than an isolated incident.

The 2024-25 accounting and auditor crisis — which included a delayed 10-K, the resignation of Supermicro's auditor, and a period of Nasdaq listing non-compliance — is resolved. The company regained Nasdaq compliance in January 2026.

Separately, and unresolved as of this writing: in March 2026, the DOJ indicted co-founder and board member Yih-Shyan Liaw, along with two others, on charges related to alleged export-control violations and chip smuggling to China. A related securities fraud class action against the company remains ongoing. Neither matter is reflected in a clean resolution, and both carry binary, stock-moving risk independent of the underlying business trajectory.
</Callout>

## Catalysts

$39B order backlog execution through FY2027. NVIDIA Vera Rubin NVL4 platform ramp. Potential resolution of the legal overhang.

## Risks

Continued margin compression from competitive "price war" dynamics among server OEMs. Dilution from the $7B equity raise. Extreme volatility, reflected in a 2.58 beta and a 52-week range spanning $19.48 to $62.36. Concentration risk from AI GPU-based platforms representing over 80% of revenue, tying growth directly to NVIDIA GPU allocation and supply. Unresolved legal exposure tied to the DOJ indictment of a co-founder and the related securities litigation, against a now three-episode pattern of governance issues.

## Status

This is an active position, underwritten on an 18-24 month thesis horizon rather than a near-term catalyst trade. The core call — that Supermicro's AI infrastructure growth is real and continuing — is intact, but position sizing and conviction are calibrated to the unresolved legal overhang and the recurring governance pattern, either of which could move the stock sharply in either direction independent of the underlying business trajectory.
